Addendum: opponent signals + bid 3 ablation

The main report established sig = 17 as the optimal single-threshold hand_power setting. Two natural extensions got asked about afterwards:

  1. Opponent-signal awareness. When partner signaled one direction and the enemy signaled the opposite, should the dealer or seat-3 player defensively take the bid rather than let the opponents call their signaled direction? Two new rules (dealer defensive take, seat-3 contested push) were added to the strategy template, gated by let thresholds so each can be ablated.
  2. Bid 3 at sig=17. At a threshold this strict, how often does bid 3 ("strong both directions") even fire? Is it vestigial, or is the threshold mis-set relative to the bid-1/bid-2 threshold? The bid-3 threshold is now a separate knob (bid3Threshold) so it can be loosened independently of the main signal.

Each change was evaluated at the same N = 20,000 games per config, same pool / seed as the main sweep, against baseline Family.

sig=17 is still the optimal threshold — the sig recheck with the new knobs enabled puts sig=17 on top, sig=16 and sig=18 below it by 0.5-1pp. But the two proposed additions behaved very differently from the hypothesis:

  • Opponent-signal defense did basically nothing. Every "defensive take" and "contested push" variant landed within noise of the baseline's 50.24% — numerically identical in most cases (same W/L count to the unit). The reason, visible from the diagnostic: the conditions the rules fire on (partner_bid != enemy_bid AND both signals were given AND hand has power in partner's direction) are extremely rare in simulation. The game just doesn't hit this state often enough for the rule to matter.
  • Bid 3 was actively costing performance, not gaining it. Simply disabling bid 3 at sig=17 moves the win rate from 50.24% (tied) to 50.87% (CI lower bound 50.18% — clearly above 50%). Bid 3 fires on only ~0.7% of hands at this threshold, and in the few cases it does fire, partner escalates or takes, and the bidding flow ends up worse than if bid 3 had simply not been an option.

The best-combined config beats Family by 0.18pp at 95% confidence, a first in this report — every previous sweep had sig=17 at a statistical tie rather than a win.

Why opponent-signal awareness didn't help

Three mechanisms contribute to the null result:

  1. Contested-signal situations are rare. "Partner signaled 1, enemy signaled 2" (or symmetric) requires both events to happen in the same hand. At sig=17 the signal fires on ~1-2% of hands per player, so the probability of both firing for partner and enemy in the same deal is on the order of 0.04%. Most of the time only one side signals, or neither does.
  2. Existing rules already handle the main case. The baseline already takes at bid 4 on max_suit_count ≥ 5 or hand_power(direction) ≥ 17. The additional "take defensively on contested signals" rule mostly fires on hands that were going to take anyway, so there's nothing incremental to gain.
  3. Family-as-opponent doesn't over-commit on contested signals either. The mental model behind the proposal was "the enemy will call their signaled direction and hurt us" — but Family's trump selection doesn't always do that, especially when the declarer's own hand disagrees with the signal. So even when we don't defensively take, we sometimes luck into the opponents calling a direction we can stop.

A future run could relax the threshold further (e.g., defensive take at hand_power ≥ 7 or fewer guards) to let the rule fire more often, but at some point we'd be taking on marginal hands where the rule creates losses rather than wins. The 20k-game sweep across thresholds of 10, 12, and 14 showed essentially no deviation from baseline, suggesting the space of "correctly defensive" opportunities is small to nonexistent for this family of rules.

Why disabling bid 3 helps

The bid-3 diagnostic (left table above) shows that at bid3=17, only 0.73% of hands fire. Of those that go on to become declarer, 70% make the contract — a good-looking number in isolation. But 70% is worse than what the same hand would achieve by bidding 2 or 4 instead:

  • The strongest bid-3-firing hands (AA + KK + QJ + mixed low cards = hp(up) ≈ 17 AND hp(down) ≈ 17) could absolutely make bid 2 (contract 8) or bid 4 (contract 10). Bid 3 (contract 9) forces partnership commitment to a 9-book contract that's actually harder to tune than bid 2 or bid 4.
  • When the signaling player bids 3, partner and enemies interpret it as "strong both directions" — a specific piece of info that's often less actionable than "strong in one direction" (bid 1 or 2). Partner can't confidently escalate to 5 because they don't know which direction to commit to.

The firing-rate table shows that loosening bid3 to 13-15 makes things worse (more firings at lower contract-make rates), and tightening above 17 is unreachable (almost no hands qualify). The correct move at sig=17 is to remove bid 3 entirely.

Does this change sig=17?

No. With bid 3 disabled (the only additive change that helps), the sig recheck shows sig=17 is still the peak:

  • sig=15: 50.14% (tied)
  • sig=16: 50.01% (tied)
  • sig=17: 50.87% (BEATS Family at p<0.05)
  • sig=18: 49.60% (tied-trending-down)
  • sig=19: 48.56% (loses)

The peak is even sharper than in the original sweep. The reason: at lower sigs, the bid-2/bid-1 signal is noisier (admits Q/J-dominant hands that don't justify the commitment), and that noise interacts with whatever bid-3 is doing. With bid 3 out of the picture, the clean Goldilocks zone at sig=17 becomes clear again.
