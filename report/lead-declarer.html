Role-aware lead variants

The Full Receiver page tested a blanket "lead strongest non-trump" rule and it lost 3.6pp to baseline. The feedback: "this is done by humans ONLY when their partner is the declarer — the assumption is that partner will support your winners". The previous implementation didn't make that distinction.

To test the nuanced form cleanly, two new DSL variables were added:

  • am_declarer — true only when playerId == declarer.
  • partner_is_declarer — true when my partner (but not me) is the declarer. Implies on_declarer_team.

The four variants below all differ only in how they gate the "lead strongest non-trump" rule. Baseline is unchanged from the rest of the report.

Interpretation

The blanket variant costs -3.57pp versus baseline. The nuanced variant (partner_is_declarer only) still loses -1.10pp. The DSL-sanity variant (on_declarer_team and not am_declarer) lands at the same point estimate within noise, confirming the new partner_is_declarer boolean matches the derived expression.

Even the nuanced gating doesn't recover baseline performance. The heuristic might be wrong as stated — or it interacts with other play rules in ways that the isolated change can't fix.

Variant rationales

KeyRationale
I_blanketThe previous implementation — a single string transform that affects ALL leading situations, regardless of declarer role. Included as a comparison baseline for the nuanced variants.
I_partner_declarerThe INTENDED heuristic: when my partner is the declarer and I have control, cash my winners top-to-bottom — partner called trump with the expectation of supporting my side suits. Uses the new `partner_is_declarer` DSL variable.
I_on_team_not_meSame behavior as partner_is_declarer expressed via the older variables. Included to sanity-check the new DSL variable matches the derived expression exactly.
I_dual_roleExplicit role-split: partner-of-declarer cashes, I-as-declarer ducks. Functionally identical to partner_is_declarer variant (the am_declarer rule just preserves existing behavior) but serves as scaffolding for role-specific future rules.
