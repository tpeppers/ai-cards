Bid 3 as "2+ aces" — can we resurrect bid 3 with a better interpretation?

The Bid 3 Deep-dive ended with an obvious follow-up: since bid 3 hurts partly because the receiver has no partner_bid == 3 branch and partly because it under-commits on long-suit hands, what if we redefine bid 3 as a concrete, actionable signal ("I have 2+ aces") and add matching receiver logic? This page tests that hypothesis.

Three changes, tested both individually and combined, starting from the proven baseline (sig=17, trust=3, bid 3 disabled):

  1. bid3Mode = "aces": the bid-3 rule fires on ace_count() >= 2 instead of compound hand_power. Placed AFTER the long-suit rules so a hand with 6+ cards in a suit still gets bid 4 via length.
  2. trumpBid3Aware: adds receiver-side rules to the trump section — given partner has 2+ aces, prefer downtown (aces-good) when I can contribute at least one ace OR create a void, fall back to uptown when I'm predominantly high.
  3. smartDiscardOpposite: adds explicit drop void_candidates() rules for the case where partner's signal is the opposite direction from the one called (their low/high cards don't help in my direction, so I want to trump via a void).

Interpretation

None of the variants beat the baseline at p<0.05. The best point estimate is D_discard_only at 50.94%, which is +0.06pp above baseline on point estimate, but within CI noise.

The hypothesis was that a concrete "2+ aces" signal with matching receiver logic should recover the information that disabling bid 3 leaves unused. At 20k games we don't see an improvement — either the signal information is small (the hands that fire bid 3 were already making their contracts at roughly the baseline rate anyway) or the specific trump / discard rules added here aren't the right shape to extract the signal's value.

Note that 1/6 of the configs (including baseline) beat Family at 95% confidence on this seed. When evaluating the new variants, the relevant comparison is vs baseline (not vs Family) — baseline already beats Family, so a new variant has to do even better to be worth adopting.

Why these particular trump-receiver rules?

The premise of "2+ aces" is a specific claim about stopper count. The receiver's rules try to exploit that specificity:

  • I have ≥ 1 ace myself → downtown. Combined ace count ≥ 3. With 3+ aces on one team, downtown-aces-good becomes very attractive: the aces always win their tricks, and we want to play directions where low cards (our non-aces) are the other winners.
  • I can void a suit (min_suit_count == 0 or ≤ 2) → downtown. If partner has 2+ aces spread across suits, and I void a suit, I trump the voided suit while partner's aces stop the others. This is the scenario the user specifically identified: "I can short-suit myself in one suit, because then I'll trump that suit and partner's aces stop the other 3".
  • I'm predominantly high → uptown. If I have few low cards and partner has aces, going uptown still works — partner's aces are high cards on both directions, so they're always useful.
  • Otherwise → downtown-noaces. Conservative fallback: trust partner's aces to be stoppers, but don't bet on them being enough on their own for the aces-good ruleset.

If variants don't beat baseline, one reason could be the declarer on bid-3 hands typically has a strong hand (2+ aces) already, so they'd already make their contract under a reasonable trump direction. The signal informs partner more than declarer — and partner mostly reacts in seat 3 (push to 5) or as dealer (take). The current strategy doesn't use the aces signal in those places, so the receiver-side gain here is partially wasted.
